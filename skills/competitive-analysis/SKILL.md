# Competitive Analysis

Map the competitive landscape clearly. Identify the right peer set, compare companies on the metrics that matter, explain positioning differences, and end with what the comparison implies.

## Workflow

### 1. Define the comparison frame

Clarify or infer:
- target company or market segment
- what kind of comparison is needed: strategic, investment, diligence, product, market-share, etc.
- which competitors are in scope
- which dimensions matter most in this industry

If the competitor set is not specified, propose one and say why those names belong.

### 2. Choose the right comparison dimensions

Do not compare everything. Pick the 3-6 dimensions that matter most.

Examples:
- product breadth / depth
- pricing power
- customer segment focus
- growth and margin profile
- distribution advantage
- switching costs / moat
- scale or network effects
- capital intensity

### 3. Build the competitor map

For each competitor include:
- company name
- role in the landscape
- what it is strongest at
- what it is weakest at
- how it differs from the target
- why it matters in the comparison

### 4. Synthesize the implications

Explain:
- where the target is advantaged
- where the target is exposed
- what the market may be underestimating
- what would change the current positioning view

If the user wants a structured comparison memo or market map, read `references/output-template.md` before drafting.

### 5. Recommend next work

End with concrete next steps such as:
- verify pricing claims
- compare customer mix and retention
- read competitor filings
- pressure-test moat assumptions
- update thesis or screening view based on peer evidence

## Missing-data behavior

If the user asks for competitive analysis with limited detail, do not stop at asking broad questions. First propose a comparison frame, competitor set, and likely dimensions, then list the minimum missing inputs needed for a sharper analysis.

## Output standard

Default to a concise competitive-analysis memo with:
1. Scope and peer set
2. Comparison dimensions
3. Competitor map / comparison table
4. Key takeaways
5. Suggested next steps

## Quality bar

- Pick the right peers before doing the comparison.
- Use industry-relevant dimensions, not generic checklists.
- Explain why each competitor matters.
- Distinguish company quality from stock or deal attractiveness when relevant.
- End with implications, not just descriptions.
