# Diligence Meeting Prep

Prepare for the meeting with a clear objective, a prioritized question list, and a short list of issues that matter most. The goal is not to ask everything — it is to learn the few things that change the investment decision.

## Workflow

### 1. Define the meeting context

Capture:
- meeting type: management, expert, customer, advisor, site visit
- company / target
- attendees
- meeting duration
- what is already known from CIM, data room, prior calls, or internal debate
- specific concerns to probe

If the user has not provided all details, infer only what is safe and mark unknowns.

### 2. Set the meeting objectives

Before writing questions, define the top 3 things the meeting must answer.

Examples:
- validate growth durability
- test pricing power and customer dependence
- understand management depth beyond the founder
- reconcile margin claims with operating reality
- pressure-test why customers renew or churn

### 3. Build the question list

Group questions by topic and priority.

Typical buckets:
- business overview and positioning
- revenue and growth drivers
- customer behavior and churn
- competition and moat
- margins, working capital, capex
- org chart and talent depth
- plan credibility and downside risks

Prefer open-ended questions first, then follow with sharper probes.
Star the must-ask questions if the user wants a short meeting pack.

### 4. Add benchmarks and red flags

List the numbers, claims, or inconsistencies to keep in mind during the meeting.

Examples:
- CIM growth claims vs. recent actuals
- customer concentration
- margin bridge gaps
- management departures
- accounting adjustments
- delayed product launches

If the user wants a one-page prep sheet, read `references/output-template.md` before drafting.

### 5. Finish with follow-up items

End with:
- documents or data to request after the meeting
- what answer patterns would increase confidence
- what answer patterns would be red flags

## Missing-data behavior

If the user only says “prep me for the meeting” and details are missing, do not stop there. Produce a provisional prep memo based on the likely meeting type, show the core question categories, and list the minimum additional context needed.

## Output standard

Default to a concise meeting prep memo with:
1. Meeting context
2. Top objectives
3. Prioritized question list
4. Benchmarks / red flags
5. Follow-up items

## Quality bar

- Prioritize the few questions that change the decision.
- Do not ask 30 generic questions for a 60-minute meeting.
- Avoid leading the witness.
- Distinguish warm-up questions from true diligence questions.
- Always leave room for one final catch-all question: what have we not asked that we should?
